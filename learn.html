Indoor solutions provide the coverage you want to be connected to—it can be Cellular, 5G, Private 5G, Wi-Fi, or Public Safety. DAS - Distributed Antenna System

DAS Basics

The fundamental concept of Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS) remains the same, but its purpose has evolved. Originally, DAS primarily addressed signal degradation and coverage issues inside structures (buildings, tunnels, garages) caused by building materials and distance from cell towers. This solved problems like dropped calls and slow speeds.

Today, while coverage remains important in challenging locations (e.g., high-rises, underground areas), capacity is the number one driver for investing in DAS.

To understand DAS solutions, they are broken down into two main components:

  • Signal Source
  • Distribution Network (the DAS itself): This is a network of spatially separated antennas linked by interconnected passive and active components, designed specifically to enhance in-building wireless signal quality.

This guide simplifies the concept of DAS, omitting complex technical details to focus on the core components used to enhance indoor wireless signals.

A Distributed Antenna System (DAS) is fundamentally composed of two main parts:

RF Signal Source

How the signal enters the system:

  • Dedicated: A direct connection to the wireless operator's own equipment (e.g., a Base Transceiver Station - BTS).
  • Off-air: Using Bi-Directional Amplifiers (BDAs) or repeaters to capture an external signal.

RF Distribution Network

How the signal is spread indoors:

  • Active Components: Head-end units, Remote Radio Units, and the Fiber distribution network.
  • Passive Components: Coaxial cable, combiners, splitters, Points of Interface (POIs), and antennas (both donor and indoor).

The distinction between Active and Passive DAS is determined by the required power for the distribution network components:

DAS Type Definition Key Components
Active DAS Uses components that require power (referred to as Active Components) between the signal source and the distribution antennas.
  • Remote Radio Units
  • Head-End interfaces
  • Fiber distribution
Passive DAS Uses components that operate without external power (referred to as Passive Components).
  • Coaxial cable
  • Splitters
  • Antennas

Connected directly to the signal source (BTS, BDA, or repeater)

It is important to note that Hybrid DAS solutions (combining Active and Passive elements) also exist.

Choosing between Active and Passive DAS is complex, and cost is not the only factor, even though active components are more expensive. Considerations such as space availability, landlord negotiations, network neutrality, future-proofing, and total cost ensure that both Active and Passive systems will remain viable options for years.

Boosters

Signal Boosters

Instead of repeating common knowledge about boosters found elsewhere, our objective is to help you make the right decision regarding your wireless coverage problem.

This introductory guide focuses on providing information that helps you choose a solution, particularly addressing issues that vendors often overlook, such as whether a booster is legal or the right solution for your specific needs.

Signal boosters (often used interchangeably with repeaters and Bi-Directional Amplifiers/BDAs) resolve indoor "dead zones" by capturing an existing cellular signal (typically from the roof or building exterior), amplifying it, and then rebroadcasting it throughout the desired coverage area. The term "booster" usually refers to smaller devices intended for limited spaces like a single home, small office, or vehicle.

The critical aspect of signal boosters is correct installation and configuration to solve problems without creating interference.

The most important requirement is that the booster owner MUST obtain approval from the licensed wireless service operator(s) whose signal will be amplified before turning the device on.

This approval is mandatory because the frequencies used by operators (cellular, public safety, etc.) are federally licensed. The operator must ensure the booster system is designed according to engineering principles to prevent interference with their network.

Note: The building owner is responsible for coordinating this approval, not the equipment seller, even if non-technical retailers sell the devices.

The key takeaway is this: If a vendor offers you a booster system without coordinating approval from the wireless service operators, you should seek a second opinion from Wireless System design experts like Linkwave Wireless.

We help clients avoid costly mistakes—such as illegal installations that operators later order to be shut down—by ensuring the system is done correctly the first time.
